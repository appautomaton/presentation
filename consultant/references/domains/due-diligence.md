# Due Diligence

How to assess a business before an acquisition, investment, or partnership. Covers scoping, analysis modules, risk synthesis, and recommendation structure.

---

## 1. DD Types and When to Emphasize Each

| Type | Prioritize when |
|---|---|
| **Commercial** | Revenue growth is the deal thesis |
| **Operational** | Cost synergies or integration complexity are key |
| **Financial** | Financial performance is the primary value driver |
| **Strategic** | Integration risk is the dominant concern |

Depth follows the deal thesis. A capability acquisition needs deep operational and talent DD. A market consolidation needs deep commercial DD. Spread effort evenly and you'll be shallow everywhere.

---

## 2. Scoping

Define boundaries before requesting data:

- **Transaction type**: Acquisition, minority investment, partnership, vendor assessment.
- **Priority areas**: Rank by deal thesis relevance. Assign H/M/L to each DD type.
- **Constraints**: Timeline (weeks available), access level (data room vs. management meetings), team capacity.
- **Key questions**: The 3-5 questions that, once answered, determine whether the deal proceeds.

## 3. Commercial Analysis

### Market assessment

| Metric | Finding | Source | Confidence |
|---|---|---|---|
| TAM | $X | Industry reports | H/M/L |
| Target's market share | X% | Company data | H/M/L |
| Market growth rate | X% CAGR | External data | H/M/L |
| Market position | #X of Y | Competitive analysis | H/M/L |

### Customer analysis

| Metric | Finding | Risk level | Trend |
|---|---|---|---|
| Top-10 customer concentration | X% of revenue | H/M/L | direction |
| Net revenue retention | X% | above/below 100% | direction |
| Gross churn rate | X% | vs. industry benchmark | direction |
| Average contract duration | X months | long/short vs. peers | direction |
| Pricing power | assessment | expanding/stable/eroding | direction |

**Customer concentration** is the single most common deal-killer in commercial DD. If the top 3 customers represent >30% of revenue, the deal is one relationship away from a material miss.

### Revenue quality

- Recurring vs. one-time: What percentage of revenue is contractually recurring?
- Revenue recognition: Any aggressive or unusual recognition policies?
- Backlog: How much revenue is committed but not yet recognized?
- Pipeline: How does the sales pipeline support the forward revenue plan?

## 4. Operational Analysis

### Technology and infrastructure

| Area | Assessment | Risk | Detail |
|---|---|---|---|
| Architecture scalability | Finding | H/M/L | Can it support 3-5x growth? |
| Technical debt | Quantified estimate | H/M/L | Remediation cost and timeline |
| IP ownership | Status | H/M/L | Patents, trade secrets, licenses |
| Security posture | Assessment | H/M/L | Last audit, certifications, incidents |
| Development velocity | Metrics | H/M/L | Deploy frequency, lead time, MTTR |

### Management and team

| Dimension | Assessment | Risk | Detail |
|---|---|---|---|
| Leadership depth | Finding | H/M/L | Bench strength below C-suite |
| Key-person dependencies | Names/roles at risk | H/M/L | Single points of failure |
| Retention risk | Assessment | H/M/L | Turnover trends, engagement, comp |
| Track record | Performance history | H/M/L | Delivery on past commitments |

## 5. Financial Analysis

### Quality of earnings

Build a reported-to-adjusted bridge for revenue, EBITDA, and net income. Key adjustments to look for: non-recurring items, owner benefits, one-time costs, and aggressive recognition timing. The adjusted number is what the business actually earns on a normalized basis.

### Working capital

Normalize for seasonality. Track AR days, AP days, and inventory days as trends, not snapshots. Identify whether the business is a net generator or consumer of working capital as it grows — this drives the cash need at close.

### Cash flow

Compare operating cash flow to EBITDA. Persistent divergence signals aggressive revenue recognition, working capital consumption, or hidden capital expenditure. Cash conversion ratio (OCF / EBITDA) should be >80% for a healthy business.

## 6. Risk Synthesis

Organize findings into three tiers:

**Critical risks (deal-killers)**: Issues that could prevent the deal from proceeding. Require resolution or walk-away.

**Major risks (deal-adjusters)**: Issues that change the valuation, structure, or terms. Price the risk into the deal.

**Minor risks (integration items)**: Issues that need attention post-close but don't affect the deal decision.

For each risk: state the risk, assess likelihood and impact, identify the mitigation, and name who owns it.

### Red flags

Patterns that warrant immediate escalation, not just documentation:
- Revenue concentrated in accounts with no contractual protection
- Key-person dependencies with no succession plan and no retention mechanism
- Material litigation undisclosed in initial representations
- Technology infrastructure that cannot scale without full replacement
- Financial results that don't reconcile across different reporting views

## 7. Recommendation Structure

The DD recommendation follows the standard evidence chain: data → finding → implication → recommendation.

```
## Recommendation

### Governing thought
[One sentence: proceed / proceed with conditions / do not proceed]

### Key strengths
1. [Strength with evidence]
2. [Strength with evidence]

### Key concerns
1. [Concern with evidence and mitigation]
2. [Concern with evidence and mitigation]

### Valuation implications
- Revenue quality adjustment: X%
- Customer concentration adjustment: Y%
- Operational risk adjustment: Z%

### Conditions precedent
- [ ] Condition 1 (required before close)
- [ ] Condition 2

### Next steps
1. [Action with owner and deadline]
2. [Action with owner and deadline]
```
