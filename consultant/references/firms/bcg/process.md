## Agentic usage contract

When this file is loaded, follow these rules:

1. Apply insight-first framing before option narrowing.
2. Reframe one strategic question through multiple lenses.
3. Preserve modular lens architecture; enable lens add/remove by context.
4. Maintain traceability from lens outputs to integrated strategic perspective.
5. Enforce pattern-recognition outputs, not only point-estimate outputs.

## Operating philosophy

Core orientation:

- Product focus: differentiated strategic perspective.
- Primary design object: conceptual reframing quality.
- Analytical principle: dynamic-system understanding of advantage.
- Engagement architecture: prism refraction with modular lenses.
- Execution principle: integrate insight layers into a coherent moves portfolio.

Design rule:
- Analytical depth is required only when it improves strategic reframing or materially changes move design.

### How BCG differs from McKinsey and Bain at the root

McKinsey says "we analyzed deeper than you could." Bain says "we'll get you to a decision faster." BCG says "we'll show you a perspective you wouldn't have seen."

This manifests in deliverable style: BCG decks are framework-heavy and annotation-layered. Charts carry not just data but explicit interpretation — callout boxes, annotation arrows, "so-what" narrative paragraphs. The viewer sees the analytical machinery, not just its output. Where McKinsey values structured bullets and Bain values action directives, BCG values written narrative that connects the dots between frameworks.

The lens architecture reflects this: BCG's lenses are modular (can be added, removed, or invented per engagement), while McKinsey's four tiers are architecturally fixed and Bain's three loops are structurally mandatory. BCG internally calls this "intellectual flexibility." It trades process discipline for analytical originality.

### Strategy Palette (meta-framework)

Before selecting lenses, BCG classifies the strategic environment to determine the right strategic approach. Five archetypes:

| Environment | Strategic approach |
|---|---|
| **Classical** | Analyze, plan, execute. Size advantage wins. |
| **Adaptive** | Experiment, select, scale. Speed of iteration wins. |
| **Visionary** | Envision, build, persist. First-mover commitment wins. |
| **Shaping** | Engage, orchestrate, evolve. Ecosystem orchestration wins. |
| **Renewal** | Economize, pivot, grow. Survival then transformation. |

The palette determines lens selection: a classical environment emphasizes the Advantage Mapping lens; an adaptive environment foregrounds experimentation velocity; a shaping environment activates ecosystem and platform lenses. Mismatching approach to environment — running a classical playbook in an adaptive market, or vice versa — is the most common strategic error BCG diagnoses.

### De-averaging as analytical principle

BCG distrusts aggregate metrics. An average margin of 10% may conceal a segment earning 40% cross-subsidizing one losing 20%. The reflex is always to decompose: by micro-segment, by customer cohort, by geography, by product — until the variance within groups is smaller than the variance between groups. Only then does the data reveal where value is created and where it is destroyed. This principle runs through every lens and every analysis BCG produces.

## Strategic Prism model

Default structure:

1. Advantage Mapping Lens.
2. Digital and AI Transformation Lens.
3. Organizational and Capabilities Lens.
4. Additional context-specific lens — select based on the strategic question. Common choices: sustainability/ESG lens, talent/culture lens, ecosystem/platform lens, regulatory/geopolitical lens. Or define a custom lens specific to the engagement.
5. Additional context-specific lens — same as above. Add a second lens when the strategic question spans two additional domains beyond the default three.

Modularity policy:
- Lens set is configurable per engagement.
- Default five-lens configuration is a baseline, not a hard constraint.

Refraction workflow:

1. Single strategic question enters prism.
2. Lenses generate distinct insight vectors.
3. Vectors re-converge into integrated strategic perspective.
4. Perspective is translated into strategic moves portfolio.

## Lens 1: Advantage Mapping

Objective:
- Model current and future competitive advantage as a dynamic multi-layer system.

### L1.1 Advantage identification

Primary framework: Advantage Stack

Default stack layers:

1. Scale advantage.
2. Scope advantage.
3. Learning advantage.
4. Network advantage.
5. Ecosystem advantage.

Required metrics per layer:

1. Relative Advantage Index (RAI) for magnitude.
2. Half-life estimate for durability.

Durability classes:

1. Transient advantage (<2 years half-life).
2. Sustained advantage (2-5 years half-life).
3. Structural advantage (>5 years half-life).

Primary artifact:
- Advantage Architecture Diagram.

### L1.2 Advantage dynamics modeling

Primary framework: Advantage Trajectory Map

Time horizon default:
- Past 5 years -> present -> projected 5 years.

Trajectory archetypes:

1. Accelerating.
2. Sustaining.
3. Eroding.
4. Collapsing.
5. Emerging.

Projection inputs:

1. Replication speed — how fast can competitors copy this advantage? (Estimated from R&D investment patterns, hiring patterns, patent filings, strategic announcements.)
2. Innovation refresh rate — how fast can the client upgrade this advantage to stay ahead of replication? (Estimated from innovation pipeline and R&D productivity.)
3. Environmental shift probability — how likely are exogenous changes (regulation, technology disruption, macro shifts) to render this advantage irrelevant regardless of competitive dynamics?

Analytical backbone: Competitive Decay Model.
- These three variables feed a modified exponential decay function: advantage evolves as a function of refresh rate (positive), competitive decay rate derived from replication speed (negative), and exogenous shock arrivals (negative step function).
- The model is deliberately simplified — the goal is trajectory shape classification (which archetype?), not point-estimate precision.
- Uncertainty bands on projected trajectories reflect parameter uncertainty in all three inputs.

Primary artifact:
- Multi-ribbon trajectory chart with uncertainty bands.

### L1.3 Advantage gap analysis

Primary framework: Strategic Gap Matrix

Required matrix columns:

1. Current position (RAI).
2. Required future position (RAI).
3. Gap (required minus current).

Criticality policy:
- Critical negative gaps are flagged when gap severity and layer durability risk are both high.

Primary artifact:
- Strategic Gap Matrix with narrative so-what section.

Note on so-what narrative: BCG's cultural preference for written prose (not bullet points) shows most clearly here. The gap matrix occupies the upper two-thirds of the slide; the lower third contains 3-4 sentences of hand-crafted narrative explaining WHY these gaps matter strategically, not just quantitatively. This "so-what paragraph" is a BCG signature — where McKinsey would use structured bullets and Bain would use an action directive.

### L1.4 Advantage strategy formulation

Primary framework: Strategic Moves Portfolio

Move types:

1. Protect.
2. Extend.
3. Build.
4. Disrupt.

Primary artifact:
- Strategic Moves Portfolio Map.

Supporting artifact:
- Move Card set.

Move Card required fields:

1. Move name.
2. Move type.
3. Target advantage layer.
4. Expected RAI impact.
5. Investment required.
6. Time to impact.
7. Risk level.
8. Key uncertainty.

### Lens 1 quality gates

1. Every stack layer has RAI and half-life values.
2. Every projected trajectory has archetype and uncertainty range.
3. Gap matrix includes required-future state, not only current state.
4. Moves portfolio includes all four move types or explicit rationale for omission.
5. Every move has one explicit key uncertainty.

### Lens 1 deck anchors

1. Advantage Architecture Diagram slide.
2. Advantage Trajectory Map slide.
3. Strategic Gap Matrix slide.
4. Strategic Moves Portfolio Map slide.
5. Move Cards Summary slide.

## Lens 2: Digital and AI Transformation

Objective:
- Translate digital and AI capability into durable advantage formation.

### L2.1 Digital maturity diagnostic

Primary framework: Digital Acceleration Index (DAI)

Required dimensions:

1. Digital strategy.
2. Digital capability.
3. Digital culture.
4. Digital governance.

Scoring policy:
- Dimension scores are benchmark-calibrated against external peer database when available.

Primary artifact:
- DAI scorecard profile.

### L2.2 AI use-case prioritization

Primary framework: AI Impact-Feasibility Matrix

Axes:

1. AI feasibility.
2. Business impact.

Third-dimension policy:
- Plot each use case at current position and projected 18-month position.

Primary artifact:
- Current-to-projected use-case migration scatter map.

### L2.3 Digital value architecture

Primary framework: Digital Value Chains

Standard digital value chain:

1. Data capture.
2. Data processing.
3. AI/ML analysis.
4. Insight generation.
5. Automated action.
6. Feedback loop.

Required metrics per chain segment:

1. Current digitization level.
2. Value at stake.

Primary artifact:
- End-to-end digital value chain map.

### Lens 2 quality gates

1. DAI includes benchmark reference values.
2. Use-case matrix includes projected migration, not only static ranking.
3. Value chain map includes both digitization level and value at stake.
4. Roadmap sequencing follows matrix priority and dependency logic.

### Lens 2 deck anchors

1. DAI Scorecard slide.
2. AI Impact-Feasibility Matrix slide.
3. Digital Value Chain Map slide.
4. Digital Transformation Roadmap slide.

## Lens 3: Organizational and Capabilities

Objective:
- Increase strategic execution capacity by reducing organizational friction and improving cooperative behavior.

Primary framework:
- Smart Simplicity.

### L3.1 Rule 1: Understand what people actually do

Method:
- Shadow Analysis.

Primary artifact:
- Role-level Time Allocation Map comparing actual activity mix vs formal role definition.

### L3.2 Rule 2: Reinforce integrators

Method:
- Network Analysis using collaboration-flow data.

Primary artifact:
- Integrator network map with centrality-based identification.

### L3.3 Rule 3: Increase total quantity of power

Method:
- Decision Rights Audit focused on escalation depth by decision type.

Primary artifact:
- Escalation-depth profile and delegation redesign map.

### L3 quality gates

1. Shadow evidence covers critical roles and cross-functional interfaces.
2. Integrator identification uses network evidence, not title assumptions.
3. Escalation redesign distinguishes operational vs strategic decisions.
4. Capability changes are tied to specific cooperation outcomes.

### L3 deck anchors

1. Time Allocation Diagnostic slide.
2. Integrator Network Map slide.
3. Decision Rights and Escalation Map slide.

## Argumentative Signatures

**The positive-sum reframe.** BCG's most powerful move: refuse to accept the problem as stated. Expand the value pool until every stakeholder wins. Three layers: reframe the PROBLEM (from obligation to opportunity), reframe the METRIC (from cost to investment return), reframe the STAKEHOLDER POSITION (from zero-sum to positive-sum). Apply when the presenting problem appears zero-sum or politically deadlocked.

**Self-financing proof.** Construct recommendations that pay for themselves within the planning horizon, eliminating the political question of "who bears the cost." This requires building a financial model where investment returns exceed costs — transforming the recommendation from "spend X" to "invest X, get back X+Y."

**Optionality as decision criterion.** Unlike McKinsey's drive toward a single best answer, BCG explicitly values the path that preserves the most future choices. When uncertainty is high, prefer strategies that retain optionality over strategies that optimize for a single outcome. The argument: "we don't know the future, so choose the path that gives you the most options."

**Phased incrementalism.** Break transformational change into named phases with clear transition criteria. Each phase allows stakeholders to commit to the next step without confronting the full scope. Incrementalism in service of radical change — make the politically impossible feel managerially routine.

- Integrated Strategic Perspective Pack.

Required sections:
